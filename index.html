Study notes + AI engineering, from Rachels, Hume, Kant, Parfit & Singer
Ethics asks whether we can reason our way to moral judgements, and whether they are objective or just opinion. The moment a model makes a decision that affects a person, someone has already chosen a moral framework, explicitly or by accident.
01 — THE OPENING QUESTION

Can we even reason about ethics?

The first question of the week: can we discuss moral issues with reason, or is it all feeling? The starting point is Rachels' minimum concept of morality: at the least, morality is the effort to guide conduct by reason while giving equal weight to the interests of everyone affected.

Hume's sceptical reply: reason is "the slave of the passions." Morality is rooted in sentiment, not pure logic.

That tension, reason versus feeling, runs through everything below. Three thinkers anchor the central question of whether moral judgements are objective or subjective.

ThinkerRough position
HumeSceptical. Moral judgements come from sentiment, not reason alone.
KantObjectivist. Moral law is discoverable by reason and binds all rational agents.
ParfitDefends objective normative truths we can reason toward.
02 — RELATIVISM

Subjectivism, culture, and where relativism breaks

Ethical subjectivism says moral claims just report feelings, so they are not objectively true or false. Cultural relativism says what is right is relative to a culture's standards. Two versions matter and they are easy to mix up:

VersionClaimDefensible?
DescriptiveCultures do disagree about moralityYes, it's just a fact
NormativeYou ought to do what your culture deems rightMuch harder

Singer pushes back on the normative version: it is not intuitive. The American founding fathers were slave owners, yet we do not say slaveholding was therefore right "for them." Cultural endorsement does not make an act right. Faced with suttee, General Napier's reply made the point sharply: his own custom was to hang men who burned women alive, so each could follow their tradition. The relativist has no neutral ground left to object from.

Reciprocity, do not do to others what you would not accept yourself, keeps surfacing as a cross-cultural moral floor, which itself cuts against strong relativism.

03 — KANT

Universalizability: would the rule survive everyone doing it?

Kant's categorical imperative: act only on a maxim you could will to become a universal law. Test the rule behind your action by imagining everyone following it. Pick a maxim and run the test.

"Break promises when convenient."
Pick a maxim to test it

This is the heart of universalizability: a moral rule has to apply to everyone in the same situation, with no special exception for me. Lying fails the test because universal lying destroys the very practice of trusting words.

04 — RELIGION & ETHICS

The Euthyphro fork

The divine command theory says an act is right because God commands it. Socrates' question to Euthyphro splits it open:

Is an act good because the gods love it?
then morality is arbitrary, they could love anything
or
Do the gods love it because it is good?
then goodness is independent, command is not the foundation
Either way, divine command struggles to be the ground of morality.

06 — WHY IT MATTERS FOR AI

Where each idea already lives in a model

01

Objective vs subjective = bias

If "fair" only meant "what this dataset's culture approved," injustice gets baked in. The relativism debate is the fairness debate.

02

RLHF is consequentialism in code

A reward function maximises an outcome. Optimise the wrong one and you get harm that "scored well," the classic consequentialist trap.

03

Universalizability = fairness test

Kant's "could this rule hold for everyone?" is just asking whether a decision rule is acceptable applied to every group, not only the majority.

04

Euthyphro = alignment

Is an output good, or just rewarded? If only rewarded, the model's "values" are arbitrary. That is the reward-hacking worry in old clothes.

05

Reciprocity = responsible AI

Fairness, accountability and treating affected people as ends not data points is Kant and Rachels wearing a governance lanyard.

Practical ethics gives an engineer vocabulary for decisions that otherwise get made by accident: whose interests the model weighs, whether it follows rules or chases outcomes, and whether its values are principled or just whatever the reward signal rewarded.
